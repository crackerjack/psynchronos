Time Zones

The first thing you want to do when experimenting with shifting time, is to eliminate as many variables as possible. Time is a slippery concept at the best of, well, times - so starting with a well understood base is vital if you don’t want to lose your marbles. The first thing you may want to do, is set the time zone on your platform to the time zone you are living in. This blog posts explains how to go about setting up the time zone on a cloud instance - in this case an Amazon EC2 (Lightsail) VPS.

By the way, I’ll try avoid awful puns in these blog posts, but the concept of Time is so prevalent in our thinking, that it pervades our idioms and phrases and it is sometimes (there you go), just unavoidable.

Linux Timezones

The first thing to do is to determine the time zone you want to use on the instance.

This will list the directory structure of the available time zones in an hierarchy. For example, within the Australia directory, there will be a list of time zones by city and region within Australia. Even though the instance I am running is physically in the Pacific Daylight Time Zone (UTC -7), and by default the time zone is set to UTC, I find it easier to relate to when I see all times in my own local time zone, which is Melbourne, Australia (UTC +10) at present. Of course there is Daylight Savings too, but that is fodder for another post, perhaps another time.
